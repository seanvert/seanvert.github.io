what is cognitive behavior therapy?

Originally was devised by Beck as a structured, short-term, present-oriented psychotherapy for depression, directed toward solving current problems and modifying dysfunctional (inaccurate and/or unhelpful) thinking and behavior. (Beck, 1964)

It has changed since that time, but all forms of it derive from Beck's original model.

Treatment is based on understanding the specific beliefs and patterns of behavior of individual patients. The therapist seeks in a variety of ways to produce cognitive change.

CBT has been adapted or patients with different levels of education and income as well as a variety of cultures and ages.

Sessions usually take 45 minutes, but it can be briefer.

core beliefs

are the most fundamental level of belief; they are global, rigid and overgeneralized.

automatic thoughts

the actual words or images that go through a person's mind, are situation specific and may be considered the most superficial level of cognition.

attitudes, rules and assumptions

core beliefs influence the development of an intermediate class of beliefs, which consists of (often unarticulated) attitudes, rules and assumptions.

They arise as people try to make sense of their environment from their early developmental stages and they can be unlearned.

conceptualization

begins at the first contact with patients and is refined at every subsequent contact. You make hypotheses about patients, based notjust on the cognitive formulation of the case, but also on the specific data patients present. You confirm, disconfirm, or modify your hypotheses as patients present new data. The conceptualization, therefore, is fluid. At strategic points, you will directly check your hypotheses and formulation with patients. Generally, if the conceptualization is on target, patients confirm that it "feels right", they agree that the picture the therapist presents truly resonates with them.

  • find out how they have already tried to solve the problem and why it didn't work.

  • use yourself as a model.

  • ask the patient to name another person who could conceivably have the same kind of problem.

  • if you're stuck, postpone the discussion.

periodic summaries

summarize what they have said in the form of the cognitive model to ensure that you have correctly identified what is most troublesome to patients, and to present it in a way that is more concise and clear.

final summary and feedback

goal: focus the patient's attention on the most important points of the session in a positive way.

following the final summary, the therapist elicits feedback about the session from the patient.

session 3 and beyond

As therapy progresses, there is a gradual shift in responsibility. Toward the end of therapy, patients themselves tend to identify their distorted thinking, devising their own homework assignments, and summarize the session.

Another gradual shift is from an emphasis on automatic thoughts to a focus on both automatic thoughts and underlying beliefs. As therapy moves into the final phase, there is another shift: preparing the patient for termination and relapse prevention.

problems with structuring therapy session

check the following

  • Have you failed to gently interrupt the patient to direct the session?

  • Have you failed to socialize the patient into treatment?

  • Have you failed to sufficiently engage the patient in treatment or to develop a strong therapeutic alliance?

therapist cognitions

interrupting the patient

to structure sessions effectively so therapy can proceed most efficiently, you need to use gentle interruption.

What if… patients get upset when you interrupt?

if they do not tell you spontaneously, ask them what was going through their minds when you noticed a negative shift. If they can't identify the reason, ask if you're interrupting too much and positively reinforce them, next you apologize. Then solve the problem, for example, by asking if they would like to talk without interruption for the next 5 or 10 minutes, at the end of which time you will summarize what they said.

socializing the patient

you must often describe, provide a rationale, and monitor with gentle, corrective feedback each of the session elements.

engaging the patient

a common difficulty arises when patients have dysfunctional beliefs that interfere with their ability to commit to working in treatment. They might not have clear goals. They might have unrealistic hopes. They might feel hopeless about their ability to solve problems, affect their life, or change. They may even fear that if they get better, their life will get worse in some way.

strengthening the therapeutic alliance

patients' unwillingness to conform to the prescribed structure because of their perceptions of and dysfunctional beliefs about themselves, about therapy, or about you.

how do you determine whether the problem is faulty socialization or reluctance in complying?

first, further socialize patients to the cbt model and by monitoring their verbal and nonverbal responses. If it is simply a problem in socialization, patients' responses are fairly neutral (or perhaps slightly self-critical), and subsequent compliance is good.

you can also have problems if you impose the structure in a controlling or demanding fashion.

typical problems with each stage of the therapy session

mood check

Patients' failure to fill out forms. If the difficulty is simply fault socialization, just review the rationale for filling them out. And determine wheter there's a practical difficulty that needs to be resolved.

When patients are annoyed by the request to fill out the forms, you can ask for their automatic thoughts when thinking about or actually filling them out.

brief update

A common difficulty arises when patients provide too detailed an account of or unfocused rambling about their week.

bridge between sessions

a problem arises when patients provide too much ot too little information.

typical difficulties in setting the agenda arise when patients fail to contribute to the agenda. They might be inadequately socialized, or they may put a special negative meaning on contributing.

Hopelessness can also be a problem, if they are feeling overwhelmed ask them to think about one thing to do first.

review of homework

put it in the agenda so you don't forget about it. Also don't go into much detail and lose focus on that day.

discussion of agenda items

Typical problems here include hopelessness, unfocused or tangential discussion, inefficient pacing, and the failure to make a therapeutic intervention.

Most of the time, merely describing a problem or identifying dysfunctional elements related to it will not improve the patient's condition.

Throughout the session, you should ask yourself: "How can I help the patient feel better by the end of the session?" "How can I help the patient have a better week?"

setting new homework

Patients are less likely to do homework when the therapist:

  • Suggests an assignment that is too difficult or is unrelated to the patient's concerns.

  • Fails to provide a good rationale.

  • Forgets to review homework assigned during previous sessions.

  • Does not stress the importance of daily homework in general and of specific assignments.

  • Does not explicitly teach the patient how to do the assignment.

  • Does not start the assignment in session, do covert rehearsal, or ask standard questions about potential obstacles that might get in the way.

  • Does not have the patient write down the homework assignment.

  • Noncollaboratively sets a homework assignment that the patient does not want to do.

If none of the above are true, then you'll try to ascertain whether patients hold dysfunctional beliefs about homework.

final summary

You will periodically make summaries throughout the session to make sure you understand what patients have been expressing. Use your notes to make a final summary.

feedback

Problems arise when patients are distressed at the end of a session and you have not left sufficient time to resolve their distress, or when patients are upset but fail to relate their distress to you. A practical solution to it is to start closing your session 5-10 minutes before the end.

teaching patients to identify automatic thoughts

techniques to elicit automatic thoughts
basic question: What was going through your mind just then?

To identify automatic thoughts:

  1. Ask this questions when you notice a shift in (or intensification of) affect during a session.

  2. Have the patient describe a problematic situation or a time during which she experienced an affect shift and ask the above question.

  3. If needed, have the patient use imagery to describe the specific situation or time in detail (as if it is happening now) and then ask the above question.

  4. If needed or desired, have the patient role-play a specific interaction with you and ask the above question.

Other questions to elicit automatic thoughts:

  1. What do you guess you were thinking about?

  2. Do you think you could have been thinking about _ or _? (Therapist provides a couple of plausible possibilities.)

  3. Were you imagining something that might happen or remembering something that did?

  4. What did this situation mean to you? (Or say about you?)

  5. Were you thinking _? (Therapist provides a thought opposite to the expected response.)

identifying emotions

Emotions are of primary importance in CBT. Intense negative emotion is painful and may be dysfunctional if it interferes with a patient's capacities.

The intensity and quality of patients' emotions make sense when you recognize the strength of their automatic thoughts and beliefs that have become activated.

It is important to acknowledge and empathize with how patients feel and refrain from challenging or disputing their emotions.

distinguish automatic thoughts from emotions

Many patients do not clearly understand the difference between their thoughts and their emotions.

Organize what the patient brings into the categories of the cognitive model: situation, automatic thought, and reaction (i.e., emotion, behavior, and physiological response).

When there is confusion, you:

  • Ignore it.

  • Address it at the time (either subtly or explicitly).

  • Address it later.

selecting key automatic thoughts

after you identify an automatic thought, you have to choose if it is worth focusing on. Is it currently distressing or dysfunctional or likely to recur?

You can ask how it changed since last week, how it happened, how did it made the patient feel emotionally and how intense it was then and now, what they have done.

Also check out for additional thoughts.

how to judge automatic thoughts

they are "ok" if

  • The patient is no longer distressed by it;

  • Patient acted in a functional way;

  • The situation was resolved;

  • Patient has a good solution if the same problem happen again.

even if a patient reports an important automatic thought, you might not focus on it

specially if:

  • You judge that doing so might impair the therapeutic relationship (e.g., you perceive that the patient is feeling invalidated).

  • The patient's level of distress is too high to evaluate his thinking.

  • There's insufficient time in the session to help the patient respond effectively to the thought.

  • You assess that it is more important to work on another element of the cognitive model (e.g., you might focus instead on solving the problematic situation, teaching the patient emotional regulation techniques, discussing more adaptive behavioral responses, or addressing the patient's physiological response).

  • You assess that it is more important to elicit and work on a dysfunctional belief underlying the automatic thought.

  • You assess that it is more important to discuss a different problem.

questioning to evaluate an automatic thought

After eliciting, determining the importance and distress, and identified the accompanying reactions of an automatic thought, you may collaboratively decide with the patient to evaluate it. You would rarely directly challenge the automatic thought, however, for three reasons:

  • You usually do not know in advance the degree to which any given automatic thought is distorted.

  • A direct challenge can lead patients to feel invalidated.

  • Challenging a cognition violates a fundamental principal of CBT, that of collaborative empiricism: You and the patient together examine the automatic thought, test its validity and/or utility, and develop a more adaptive response.

Patients need a structured method to evaluate their thinking.

not all questions of Socratic questioning are suitable for every automatic thought

Patients may not evaluate their thoughts at all if they consider the process too burdensome. Usually you'll go one or a few questions at a time.

the process

  • Examine the validity of the automatic thought.

  • Explore the possibility of other interpretations or viewpoints.

  • Decatastrophize the problematic situation.

  • Recognize the impact of believing the automatic thought.

  • Gain distance from the thought.

  • Take steps to solve the problem.

the "evidence" questions

Automatic thoughts contain a grain of truth, patients usually have some evidence that supports their accuracy (got first after these), but they'll often fail to recognize evidence to the contrary (which you'll seek second).

the "alternative explanation" questions

Devise a reasonable alternative explanation.

the "decatastrophizing" questions

Many patients predict a worst-case scenario. If a patient's automatic thought does not contain a catastrophe, it is often useful to ask the patients about their worst fear. In both cases, you should follow up by asking patients what they can do if the worst does happen.

They are often unrealistic. You have to help them think of more realistic outcomes. You can start asking about the best outcome.

What if… patients' worst fears are that they will die?

ask about the best and more realistic outcomes. You can also explore what the worst part of dying would be.

the "impact of the automatic thought" questions

Help patients assess the consequences of responding and not responding to their distorted thinking.

the "distancing" questions

Patients often benefit from getting some distance from their thoughts by imagining what they would tell a close friend or family member in a similar situation.

the "problem-solving" questions

The answer to this question may be cognitive and/or behavioral in nature. The cognitive part would entail having patients remember their responses to the questions.

assessing the outcome of the evaluation process

Check how the patient now believes in the original automatic thought and how they feel emotionally.

conceptualizing why the evaluation of an automatic thought was ineffective

Common reasons to consider include:

  1. There are other, more central automatic thoughts and/or images left unidentified or unevaluated.

  2. The evaluation of the automatic thought is implausible, superficial, or inadequate.

  3. The patient has not sufficiently expressed the evidence that he or she believes supports the automatic thought.

  4. The automatic thought itself is also a core belief.

  5. The patient understands intellectually that the automatic thought is distorted, but does not believe it on an emotional level.

using alternate methods to help patients examine their thinking

using alternative questions

Start questioning the validity of the thought, shift the emphasis to the implicit underlying belief. Then ask an open-ended question about it.

identifying cognitive distortions

Often there is a systematic negative bias in the cognitive processing of patients who suffer from a psychiatric disorder. It often helps to label distortions and to teach patients to do the same.

thinking errors

Although some automatic thoughts are true, many are either untrue or have just a grain of truth. Typical mistakes in thinking include:

All-or-nothing thinking

AKA black-and-white, polarized, or dichotomous thinking. You view a situation in only two categories instead of on a continuum.

Catastrophizing

AKA fortune-telling You predict the future negatively without considering other, more likely outcomes.

Disqualifying or discounting the positive

You unreasonably tell yourself that positive experiences, deeds, or qualities do not count.

Emotional reasoning

You think something must be true because you "feel" (actually believe) it so strongly, ignoring or discounting evidence to the contrary.

Labeling

You put a fixed, global label on yourself or others without considering that the evidence might more reasonably lead to a less disastrous conclusion.

Magnification/minimization

When you evaluate yourself, another person, or a situation, you unreasonably magnify the negative and/or minimize the positive.

Mental filter

AKA selective abstraction You pay undue attention to one negative detail instead of seeing the whole picture.

Mind reading

You believe you know what others are thinking, failing to consider other, more likely possibilities.

Overgeneralization

You make a sweeping negative conclusion that goes far beyond the current situation.

Personalization

You believe others are behaving negatively because of you, without considering more plausible explanations for their behavior.

"Should" and "must" statements

AKA imperatives You have a precise, fixed idea of how you or others should behave, and you overestimate how bad it is that these expectations are not met.

Tunnel vision

You only see the negative aspects of a situation.

using self-disclosure

At times, you might use judicious self-disclosure instead of or in addition to Socratic questioning or other methods, to demonstrate how you were able to change similar automatic thoughts that you had.

when automatic thoughts are true

focus on problem solving

Not all problems can be solved, but if a patient's perception of a situation appears to be valid, you might investigate whether the problem can be solved, at least to some degree.

investigate invalid conclusions

While an automatic thought might be true, the meaning may be invalid or at least not completely valid.

educating patients about beliefs

There is a range of potential beliefs that the patient could adopt, and that beliefs are learned, not innate, and can be revised.

changing rules and attitudes into assumption form

It is easier for patients to see the distortion in and test an intermediate belief that is in the form of an assumption, rather than an intermediate belief that is in the form of a rule or an attitude.

examining advantages and disadvantages of beliefs

it is useful for patients to check the pros and cons of continuing to hold a given belief. You then minimize the advantages and reinforce the disadvantages.

formulating a new belief

First, ask yourself, "what belief would be more functional for the patient?"

Before you try to modify a patient's belief, you confirm that it is central, strongly held belief, and you formulate in your own mind a more functional, less rigid belief that is thematically related to the dysfunctional one, but which you believe is more realistic and adaptive for the patient.

formulation of more functional beliefs

modifying beliefs

Some may be easy to change, but most take diligent effort. You continue to ask patients how much they still believe a given belief (0-100%), at both an "intellectual" and "emotional" level.

Generally, beliefs have been sufficiently attenuated when patients endorse them less than 30% and when they are likely to continue modifying their dysfunctional behavior despite still holding on to a remnant of the belief.

A typical homework assignment is to read and rerate daily how strongly the patient endorses the old and new beliefs.

identifying and modifying core beliefs

negative core beliefs fall into three broad categories

  • helplessness

  • unlovability

  • worthlessness

you may not succeed with early belief modification, though, if patients:

  • Have core beliefs that are quite rigid and overgeneralized.

  • Do not yet believe that cognitions are ideas and not necessarily truths.

  • Experience very high levels of affect when beliefs are elicited or questioned.

  • Do not have a strong enough alliance with you (they may not trust you sufficiently; they may not perceive you as understanding who they really are; they may feel invalidated by the process of belief evaluation).

    In such cases, you'll deal with automatic thoughts and intermediate beliefs before the core beliefs

You'll do the following during treatment

  1. Mentally hypothesize from which category of core belief specific automatic thoughts appear to have arisen.

  2. Specify the core belief (to yourself) using the same techniques you use to identify patients' intermediate beliefs.

  3. Present your hypothesis about the core belief to patients, asking for confirmation or disconfirmation; refine your hypothesis about the core belief as patients provide additional data about current and childhood situations and their reactions to them.

  4. Educate patients about core beliefs in general and about their specific core beliefs; guide patients in monitoring the operation of the core belief in the present.

  5. Help patients specify and strengthen a new, more adaptive core belief.

  6. Begin to evaluate and modify the negative core belief with patients; examine the childhood origin of the core belief (if applicable), its maintenance through the years, and its contribution to patients' present difficulties; continue to monitor the activation of the core belief in the present; use both "intellectual" and "emotional" or experimental methods to decrease the strength of the old core belief and to increase the strength of the new core belief.

categorizing core beliefs

You first make a gross distinction (to yourself) among cognitions that seem to fall in the helpless, unlovable, or worthless categories.

identifying core beliefs

Use the same techniques of intermediate beliefs.

You can try to help patients evaluate core beliefs early on. In some cases, such early evaluation is ineffective but helps you test the strength and malleability of the core belief.

presenting core beliefs

When you believe that you have collected sufficient data to hypothesize about the core belief, and when you believe patients will be sufficiently receptive, you tentatively pose your conceptualization to them.

educating patients about core beliefs and monitoring their operation

It is important that patients understand the following about core beliefs:

  • That is is an idea, not necessarily a truth.

  • That they can believe it quite strongly, even "feel" it to be true, and yet it might be mostly or entirely untrue.

  • That, as an idea, it can be tested.

  • That it may be rooted in childhood events, and may or may not have been true at the time they first came to believe it.

  • That it continues to be maintained through the operation of their schemas, in which they readily recognize data that support the core belief while ignoring or discounting data to the contrary.

  • That you and they, working together, can use a variety of strategies over time to change this idea so that they can view themselves in a more realistic way.

developing a new core belief

Many depressed patients had a different core belief, a more positive, reality-based, and functional idea about themselves, before the onset of their Axis I disorder.

strengthening new core beliefs

You'll do this in two ways, the first one was described before; the second one is strengthening the new core belief, you ask patients to examine their experiences in a new way that facilitates their ability to recognize positive data themselves.

Most patients need a visual or audio cue to remind themselves to look for positive data throughout the day, for example, they can wear a rubber band around their wrist, post sticky notes, have pop-up reminders on their computers or smartphones, or set the alarm on their cell phone to ring periodically.

other ways of having patients recognize positive data include

  1. To think of data they would say were positive evidence for another person.

  2. To name another person who would say the data indicate positive evidence about the patient.

  3. To reflect on whether they would discount specific positive evidence if they compared what they did to a hypothetical negative model.

  4. To rate the strength of their beliefs at the intellectual and emotional levels at the beginning of each session, before setting the agenda.

    using extreme contrasts to modify core beliefs

It can be helpful for patients to compare themselves with someone who is at a negative extreme of the quality related to their core belief.

using stories, movies and metaphors

When patients experience vivid examples of the invalidity of others' very strongly held core beliefs, they begin to understand how they, too, could have a very strong core belief that is not accurate.

historical tests of the core belief

When patients' core beliefs stem from early experiences, it is often useful to have them examine how their belief originated and was maintained through the years.

restructuring early memories

The "intellectual" techniques presented until now will suffice for many Axis I patients. For others, special "emotional" or experimental techniques, in which patients' affect is aroused, are also indicated.

another technique Gestalt

  1. Identify a specific situation that is currently quite distressing to the patient and seems linked to an important core belief.

  2. Heighten the patient's affect by focusing on automatic thoughts, emotions, and somatic sensations linked with this situation.

  3. Help the patient identify and reexperience a relevant early experience.

  4. Talk to "younger" part of the patient to identify automatic thoughts, emotions and beliefs.

  5. Help the patient develop a different understanding of the experience through guided imagery, Socratic questioning, dialogue, and/or role play.

additional cognitive and behavioral techniques

problem solving and skills training

You will encourage patients to put distressing problems that come up during the week in the agenda. And reinforce them to devise solutions for their problems.

making decisions

Some have difficulty making decisions, ask them to list the pros and cons of each option, then help them devise a system to weight each item.

refocusing

Is useful when concentration is needed for the task at hand or when patients are having obsessive thoughts for which rational evaluation is ineffective. You'll teach patients to label and accept their experience.

when that doesn't work

Distracting activities can be useful. While is it not an ultimate solution, it can be a helpful short-term technique.

when patients are distracting themselves too much

Patients may use distraction to avoid the experience of negative emotion. It is imperative for them to fully understand that they do not need to distract themselves because while feeling upset can feel painful, it is not harmful.

measuring moods and behavior using the activity chart

For some patients it is helpful to use the activity chart, not to schedule activities, but to monitor their moods while engaged in various activities, to look for patterns of occurrence.

Patients with a problematic behavior might record all their activities to investigate patterns of occurrence, or might just record the maladaptive behavior themselves.

relaxing and mindfulness

You should teach relaxation exercises in session, where you can deal with problems and assess efficacy. Be aware that some patients experience a paradoxical arousal effect from relaxation exercises.

graded task assignments

Patients tend to become overwhelmed when they focus on how far they are from a goal, intead of focusing on their current step to reach it.

exposure

Depressed and anxious patients often engage in avoidance, a coping strategy. While avoidance tends to bring immediate relief, it perpetuates the problem. Patients do not get the opportunity to test their automatic thoughts and receive disconfirming data.

Help them identify an activity that is associated with low to moderate discomfort and ask them to engage in this activity everyday, until their anxiety has decreased significantly.

Try to make them record their "predictions" to disqualify them as they get exposed to the activity.

role-playing

It can be useful in learning and practicing social skills.

Before teaching patients social skills, you should assess the level of skill they already possess. Many patients know precisely what to do and say, but have difficulty using this knowledge because of dysfunctional assumptions.

using the "pie" technique

It is often useful to present patients' ideas in graphical form.

setting goals

This is good to identify problems and priorities to show how imbalanced their lives can currently be from their ideas.

determining responsibility

Allows the patient to see graphically the possible causes for a given outcome.

self-comparisons and credit lists

Patients with psychiatric disorders have a negative bias in information processing, especially when evaluating themselves.

They compare themselves at present with how they were before the onset of their disorder, or with how they would like to be, or they compare themselves with others who do not have a psychiatric disorder.

changing the self-comparison

Teach them to make more functional comparisons (with herself at her worst point) and to keep a credit list.

credit lists

Are simply daily lists of positive things the patient is doing or items she deserves credit for.

Completing credit lists early in treatment also helps prepare patients for the later task of uncovering positive data for the Core Belief Worksheet.

imagery

Goals:

  • How to teach patients to identify their spontaneous images.

  • How to intervene therapeutically with both espontanous and induced images.

    identifying images

You'll need to be alert to the possibility of patients' thoughts occurring in imaginal form, because few patients spontaneously report images and some have difficulty identifying them even when prompted.

You can ask patients about synonyms, sometime patients fail to grasp what the therapist means by image.

educating patients about imagery

Some patients don't report images because they are graphic and distressing. They might be reluctant to reexperience the distress or fear you will view them as disturbed. If you suspect either scenario, normalize the experience of images. This can help patients to reduce their anxiety and makes more likely they will be able to identify their images.

responding to spontaneous images

following images to completion

This is often the most helpful. You will encourage patients to experience the spontaneous image until one of the two happens: Either patients imagine getting over the crisis, or they imagine an ultimate catastrophe (In this case, you can then explore the feared consequences and meaning of the ultimate catastrophe and intervene further).

summary

Two outcomes are possible when following an image to completion. In one instance, the problem is eventually resolved, and the patient feels relief. In the second stance, the problem worsens to a catastrophe, at which point you ask for the meaning of or the worst part of about the image, which helps you uncover a new problem.

jumping ahead of time

You might suggest that patients imagine themselves at some point in the near future.

coping in the image

Another technique is to guide patients so they can imagine they are coping with a difficult situation they have spontaneously envisioned.

changing the image

Another technique involves teaching patients to reimagine a spontaneous image, changing the ending to alleviate their distress. The first example is a realistic change, the second, a "magical" change.

reality testing the image

Here you teach patients to treat images like verbal automatic thoughts, using standard Socratic questioning.

repeating the image

You suggest that they keep imagining the original image and their level of distress change. Some patients seem to do an automatic reality check, and envision each succeding image more realistically and with less dysphoria.

substituting images

Substituting a more pleasant image has been extensively described elsewhere. It, too, must be regularly practiced in order for the patient to experience relief from distressing spontaneous images.

inducing imagery as a therapeutic tool

At times, you will induce an image as opposed to help a patient respond a spontaneous image. Covert rehearsal to uncover obstacles related to homework is one example.

rehearsal of coping techniques

This technique differs from "coping in the image" because here you induce an image rather than having patients imagine how they would cope in a spontaneous image.

distancing

Distancing is another induced imagery technique to reduce distress and help patients see problems in greater perspective.

reduction of perceived threat

A third type of induced image is designed to allow patient to view a situation with a more realistic assessment of actual threat.

homework

Homework is an integral, not optional, part of cognitive behavior therapy.

Good homework assignments provide opportunities for patients to educate themselves further, to collect data, to test their thoughts and beliefs, to modify their thinking, to practice cognitive and behavioral tools, and to experiment with new behaviors.

Take care to:

  • Tailor assignments to the individual,

  • Provide a sound rationale,

  • Uncover potential obstacles, and

  • Modify relevant beliefs.

    setting homework assignments

You may need to take the lead in suggesting homework assignments at the beginning of treatment but you begin, as soon as possible, to ask patients to devise their own assignments. Patients who routinely set their own homework by the end of therapy are more likely to continue doing so when treatment has ended.

ongoing homework assignments

  2. Monitoring automatic thoughts

  3. Evaluating and responding to automatic thoughts

  4. Problem solving

  5. Behavioral skills

  6. Behavioral experiments

a sampling of homework assignment for Sally

Some assignments below are helpful for almost any depressed patient; others were specifically designed for this particular patient.

increasing homework adherence

Implementation of the following guidelines increases the likelihood that patients will be successful with homework and experience an elevation in mood:

  1. Tailor the assignment to the individual. Err on the side of devising assignments that are too easy rather than too hard.

  2. Provide a rationale as to how and why the assignment might help.

  3. Set homework collaboratively; seek the patient's input and agreement.

  4. Make homework a no-lose proposition.

  5. Begin the assignment in session.

tailoring homework to the individual

Successful completion of homework can speed up therapy and lead to an increased sense of mastery and improved mood. Rather than suggesting assignments according to a prescribed formula, you should consider the patient's characteristics (mentioned in the introduction to this chapter) and desires.

providing a rationale

Patients are more likely to comply with homework assignments if they understand the reason for doing them.

setting homework collaboratively

Ideally, patients would set their oen homework, but at the beginning of treatment they do not know which assignments would be beneficial.

making homework a no-lose proposition

It is helpful when initially setting up assignments to stress that useful data can be obtained even when patients fail to complete their homework.

starting homework in the session

Continuing an assignment is much easier than initiating one.

remembering do to homework

It is vital to write down, or have patients write down, their assignments every week, starting with the first session.

Ask them where they will keep their annotations and how likely they think they might be to forget to look at the list on one or more days.

You can ask patients to:

  • Pair an assignment with another daily activity.

  • Post notes on their refrigerator, bathroom mirror, or the dashboard of their car.

  • Use their appointment book, smartphone, or computer to cue them.

  • Ask another person to cue them.

anticipating problems

To check how reasonable the assignments are, consider the following:

  • Is the amount and degree of difficulty of homework reasonable for this patient, or will it feel overwhelming?

  • Is it related to the patient's goals?

  • How likely is the patient to do it?

  • What practical problems may get in the way (time, energy, opportunity)?

  • What thoughts may get in the way?

If patients are unsure about the chances of completing their homework consider these strategies.

covert rehearsal

uses induced imagery to uncover and solve potential homework-related problems.

changing the assignment

Changing the assignment may be indicated if you judge that an assignment is inappropriate or if covert rehearsal has not been sufficiently effective.

You can also collaboratively decide to make certain assignments optional or to decrease the frequency or duration of a homework task. Less homework is better than no homework at all.

intellectual-emotional role-play

An intellectual-emotional role play may help motivate reluctant patients when the therapist judges it is quite important for a patient to do a given assignment

preparing for a possible negative outcome

When devising a behavioral experiment or testing an assumption, it is important to set up a scenario that is likely to succeed.

conceptualizing difficulties

practical problems

Most practical problems can be avoided if you carefully set the assingment and prepare the patient to do it. Covert rehearsal can also ferret out potential difficulties.

doing therapy homework in the last minute

Often, this avoidance is part of a larger problem, and you may first have to help patients identify and modify certain beliefs. Other patients, however, need only a gentle reminder to look at their homework list daily.

forgetting the rationale for an assignment

Occasionally, patients neglect an assignment because they do not remember why they were asked to do it. This can be avoided by having patients, who struggle on this aspect, record the rationale next to an assignment.

disorganization or lack of accountability

Many patients are more likely to do assignments when they have to mark off a daily checklist indicating wheter they fulfilled an assignment.

difficulty with an assignment

If you realize at a subsequent session that a homework assignment was too difficult or ill-defined, take responsibility. Otherwise patients may unfairly criticize themselves for not having successfully completed an assignment.

Here you have an opportunity to:

  1. Model that you can make and admit mistakes;

  2. build rapport;

  3. demonstrate that you are concerned with tailoring therapy - and homework assignments - to the patient;

  4. help patients see an alternative explanation for their lack of success.

psychological problems
negative predictions

When patients are in psychological distress, and particularly when they are depressed, they tend to assume negative outcomes. To identify dysfunctional cognitions that interfered with doing a homework assignment, ask patients to recall a specific time they thought about doing the assignment and then elicit related cognitions and feelings.

overestimating the demands of an assignment

Some patients overestimate how inconvenient or difficult it will be to do homework or do not realize that doing a therapy assignment will be time limited.

perfectionism

Many patients benefit from a simple reminder that they should not strive for perfection when doing homework.

Other patients with a strong underlying assumption about the necessity of being perfect may benefit from assignments that include mistakes.

psychological obstacles masked as practical problems

If you believe that a thought of belief is also interfering, you should investigate this possibility before discussing the practical problem.

problems related to the therapist's cognitions

Finally, you should assess whether any of your thoughts or beliefs hinder you from assertively and appropriately encouraging a patient to do homework.

reviewing homework

Before each session, you should prepare by reviewing the notes of the previous session and noting the patient's homework. A discussion of homework is typically the first item on the agenda, unless it is clearly inappropriate.

termination and relapse prevention

The goal in cognitive behavior therapy is to facilitate remission of patients' disorders and to teach them skills they can use throughout their lifetime.

early activities

You will begin to prepare patients for termination and relapse even in the initial session, as you tell them that your goal is to make treatment as time limited as possible, with the aim of helping them become their own therapist.

activities throughout therapy

attributing progress to the patient

You should be alert at every session for opportunities to reinforce patients for their progress.

Some patients initially believe that all the credit for feeling better goes to their therapist. An alternative attribution - that the patient is responsible for positive changes - can fortify the patient's sense of self-efficacy.

teaching and using tools/techniques learned in therapy

You will stress that these are lifelong aids the patient can use in a number of situations now and in the future.

Common techniques and tools that can be used during and after therapy include the following:

  1. Breaking down big problems into manageable components.

  2. Brainstorming solutions to problems.

  3. Identifying, testing, and responding to automatic thoughts and beliefs.

  4. Using Thought Records.

  5. Monitoring and scheduling activities.

  6. Doing relaxation exercises.

  7. Using distraction and refocusing techniques.

  8. Creating hierarchies of avoided tasks or situations.

  9. Writing credit lists.

  10. Identifying advantages and disadvantages (of specific thoughts, beliefs, behaviors, or choices when making a decision).

preparing for setbacks during therapy

As soon as patients begin to feel better, you will prepare them for a potential setback by asking them to imagine what will go through their mind if they start to feel worse.

Coping cards and the Progress in Therapy graph can help avoid these negative cognitions.

near termination activities

responding to concerns about tapering sessions

Several weeks before termination, you should discuss tapering sessions, as an experiment, from once a week to once every week. If patients feel anxious about it, you can list verbally or write down the advantages of reducing the number of sessions.

responding to concerns about termination

As termination approaches, it is important to elicit patients' automatic thoughts about termination. Some patients are excited and hopeful. At the other extreme, some patients are fearful or even angry. Most have some mixed feelings. They are pleased with their progress but concerned about relapse. Often they are sorry to end their relationship with you.

It is important both to acknowledge what patients are feeling and help them respond to any distortions. It is often desirable for you to express your own genuine feelings.

reviewing what was learned in therapy

You will encourage patients to read through and organize their notes so they can easily refer to them in the future.

self-therapy sessions

Although many patients do not follow through with formal self-therapy sessions, it is nevertheless useful to discuss a self-therapy plan andto encourage its use.

preparing for setbacks after termination

As mentioned previously, you begin preparing patients for setbacks early in treatment. As you near termination, you should encourage them to compose a coping card specifying what to do if a setback occurs after the therapy has ended.

guide to self-therapy sessions

  1. Reviewof past week(s)

    • What positive things have happened? What do I deserve credit for?

    • What problems came up? What did I do? If the problem recurs, what, if anything, should I do differently?

  2. Review of homework

    • Did I do what I had planned? If not, what got in the way (practical problems; automatic thoughts), and what can I do about that next time?

    • What should I continue to do this week?

  3. Current problematic issues/situations

    • Am I viewing this problem realistically, or have I been overreacting? Is there another way of iewing this?

    • What should I do?

  4. Prediction of future problems

    • What problems may come up in the next few days or weeks, and what should I do about them?

  5. Set new homework

    • What homework would be helpful? Should I consider:

      • Doing thought records?

      • Scheduling pleasure or mastery activities?

      • Reading therapy notes?

      • Practicing skills such as relaxation?

      • Doing a credit list?

  6. Schedule the next self-therapy appointment

booster sessions

You should encourage patients to schedule booster sessions after termination for several reasons. If any difficulties have arisen, you can discuss how patients handled them and assess whether they could have handled them in a better way.

Booster sessions also afford you the opportunity to check on the reemergence of dysfunctional strategies.

accomplishing broad therapeutic goals

To achieve your objectives, you will:

  1. Build a sound therapeutic alliance with patients.

  2. Make the structure and the process of therapy explicit.

  3. Teach patients the cognitive model and share your conceptualization with them.

  4. Help alleviate their distress through a variety of techniques and problem solving.

  5. Teach them how to use these techniques themselves, help them generalize the use of the techniques, and motivate them to use the techniques in the future.
